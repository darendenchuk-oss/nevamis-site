# Nevamis Client Discovery Plan

## Interview goal

Before committing to major development, talk to current or likely customers and confirm real problems. The goal is to learn what actually happens in their business today: what breaks, how often, what it costs, and how they cope with it now. We are not pitching. We are gathering evidence so we only build services that solve frequent, expensive, verifiable problems.

## Interview questions

Ask these in every interview:

1. What happens after a new call or form arrives?
2. How quickly does someone respond?
3. Where are leads recorded?
4. How many quotes remain open?
5. What causes no-shows?
6. How are reviews requested?
7. How are past customers contacted?
8. Which inbox consumes the most time?
9. Which reports does the owner manually assemble?
10. Which software holds customer and job information?
11. Which process causes the most lost revenue?
12. Which process consumes the most administrative time?
13. Which customer communications require owner approval?
14. What would make an automated system unacceptable?
15. What measurable result would justify a monthly fee?

## What to track per interview

Record these for every conversation:

- **Problem frequency**: how often the problem happens (per day, week, or month).
- **Time spent**: hours lost to the problem or the manual workaround.
- **Revenue at risk**: dollars lost to missed calls, dead quotes, no-shows, or lapsed customers.
- **Current software**: what tools they already use for customers, jobs, and scheduling.
- **Current workaround**: how they handle the problem today, even if it is a sticky note.
- **Buying authority**: whether the person we spoke to can approve a purchase, and if not, who can.
- **Urgency**: how badly they want this fixed now versus someday.
- **Willingness to pilot**: whether they would test an early version with us.
- **Required safeguards**: what an automated system must never do for them to accept it.

## What not to ask

Do not ask "Would you buy AI?"

Hypothetical buying questions produce polite, useless answers. Instead, ask about existing problems, costs, frequency, consequences, and current workarounds. Real behavior and real numbers are the only evidence that counts.

## Product validation ladder

Before building each service, work through all ten steps in order:

1. Confirm the problem through at least several relevant conversations.
2. Define one narrow workflow.
3. Define the client's current baseline.
4. Define one primary outcome.
5. Build a manual or assisted version first.
6. Test with a small number of qualified clients.
7. Measure support time and failure rate.
8. Measure client usage and outcome.
9. Determine whether the workflow is repeatable.
10. Productize only after repeated evidence.

Do not build a large platform based only on verbal enthusiasm.
